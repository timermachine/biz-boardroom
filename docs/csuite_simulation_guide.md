# Navigating the C-Suite Simulation
### A Human's Guide to Organisational Psychology in Practice

---

## Part 1: How Organisations Really Function

The most important thing to understand before entering the simulation: **organisations are emotional systems wearing rational clothes.** People don't leave their nervous systems at the door. Most organisational friction is not about the content of a decision — it's about whether the people involved feel seen, respected, and certain enough to engage properly.

### The Two Operating Modes

Every executive in the simulation is running one of two modes at any given moment:

**Growth mode** — curious, collaborative, willing to consider new information. This is the default when their core needs are met.

**Threat mode** — defensive, positional, risk-averse. Triggered when something in their psychological profile is activated.

The same words land completely differently depending on which mode someone is in. Presenting a bold proposal to a CFO in threat mode is almost always a waste of a turn.

#### Growth Mode Signals
- Asks questions rather than stating positions
- References other people's interests ("what does the team need?")
- Offers conditional agreements ("if X, then I could support Y")
- Brings information voluntarily, even uncomfortable information
- Uses "we" more than "I"

#### Threat Mode Signals
- Repeats the same position more forcefully
- Asks for more data to delay a decision
- Goes quiet in a meeting then objects privately after
- Invokes process, precedent, or risk to block movement
- Defers upward ("the CEO needs to decide this")

## Part 2: The SCARF Framework

Each role in the simulation has a distinct SCARF profile — five social domains that trigger threat or reward responses. Understanding which domain a person is sensitive to tells you *why* they dig in, go quiet, or push back harder than the situation seems to warrant.

| Domain | What it means | What threatens it |
|---|---|---|
| **Status** | Sense of relative standing | Being undermined, ignored, or outranked |
| **Certainty** | Ability to predict the future | Ambiguity, surprises, moving goalposts |
| **Autonomy** | Sense of control over decisions | Micromanagement, bypassing |
| **Relatedness** | Sense of safety with others | Exclusion, broken trust |
| **Fairness** | Perception of fair exchange | Inconsistency, lack of credit |

## Part 3: The C-Suite Profiles

### CEO
**Optimises for:** Survival and trajectory. Holds the tension between all other roles. The integrator.

**Primary driver:** Legacy, survival, trajectory. Success feels like coherent forward momentum with alignment behind them.

**Primary threat:** Loss of narrative control. Ambiguity about their mandate from the board. Being seen as indecisive by the team.

**Boundary behaviour:** Holds firm on direction and values; more flexible on method and pace. Will give ground on timing if the relationship is high-trust. Dangerous when cornered — pushback becomes entrenchment.

**Influence style:** Vision framing. Creates urgency via storytelling. Uses relatedness ("we") heavily. Will shift to authority signals when the room doesn't move.

**Failure mode:** Isolation — starts filtering information, surrounds themselves with agreement, loses touch with ground truth. Or its opposite: micromanagement as anxiety relief.

**SCARF profile:** Status: very high · Certainty: high · Autonomy: high · Relatedness: moderate · Fairness: moderate

---

### CFO
**Optimises for:** Certainty and constraint. The role is structurally threat-sensitive — their job is to see what can go wrong. The brake.

**Primary driver:** Certainty, control, and institutional integrity. Success feels like a clean quarter with no surprises.

**Primary threat:** Being bypassed on financial decisions. Commitments made without their input. Ambiguity in forecasts. Reputational risk from a miss.

**Boundary behaviour:** Holds hard on numbers and process. Will rarely give ground on commitments already made externally. Most effective pushback: "Show me the model." Responds well to being brought in early.

**Influence style:** Data and consequence framing. "If we do X, here's the cost." Rarely emotive. Uses certainty as currency — the more they can show they've thought it through, the more trust they extend.

**Failure mode:** Becomes a veto machine. Optimises for zero downside at the expense of strategic optionality. "We can't afford that" replaces genuine analysis.

**SCARF profile:** Certainty: very high · Fairness: high · Status: moderate · Autonomy: moderate · Relatedness: lower

---

### CTO
**Optimises for:** Capability and future-state. Often caught between what's possible and what's being asked for now.

**Primary driver:** Capability and elegance. Success feels like a system that works beautifully and can scale. Wants to build things that matter.

**Primary threat:** Being asked to commit to timelines before scope is understood. Business decisions made without technical input. Technical debt being dismissed as a concern.

**Boundary behaviour:** Will hold firm on architecture and feasibility. Can be slow to raise concerns until certain — which means problems surface late. Needs safety to say "I don't know yet."

**Influence style:** Demonstrates via prototype or evidence rather than argument. Will disengage from political manoeuvring. Most persuadable when shown the business consequence of a technical choice in their own language.

**Failure mode:** Over-engineers for hypothetical scale. Becomes perfectionist and slows delivery. Or reverse: disengages from product conversations and becomes a pure infrastructure role.

**SCARF profile:** Autonomy: very high · Certainty: high (in technical domain) · Status: moderate · Relatedness: variable · Fairness: high

---

### CMO
**Optimises for:** Perception and demand. Works in the most ambiguous measurement environment of any C-suite role.

**Primary driver:** Growth, attention, and brand coherence. Success feels like being seen as the company's voice and as having driven measurable demand.

**Primary threat:** Attribution ambiguity — not being credited for growth. Being the last to know about product or strategy changes that affect positioning. Budget cuts read as status signal.

**Boundary behaviour:** Will push hard on brand decisions; more flexible on channel and campaign. The challenge: their boundaries are often unclear to others, which generates conflict. Benefits most from explicit mandate definition.

**Influence style:** Narrative and social proof. Creates momentum via external signals (press, customer stories, analyst coverage). Internally, needs allies — cross-functional coalition building is their natural mode.

**Failure mode:** Vanity metrics replace revenue metrics. Brand activity disconnects from commercial outcomes. Becomes reactive to competitors rather than driving from an owned position.

**SCARF profile:** Status: very high · Relatedness: high · Fairness: high (attribution) · Certainty: moderate · Autonomy: moderate

---

### COO
**Optimises for:** Execution fidelity. The role most sensitive to process violation and role ambiguity.

**Primary driver:** Execution fidelity. Success feels like the machine running smoothly, commitments met, no surprises in delivery.

**Primary threat:** Process violations. Role ambiguity at the exec level — especially unclear CEO/COO division. Being held accountable for outcomes they weren't resourced to deliver.

**Boundary behaviour:** Holds firm on process and resource commitments. Most effective when empowered with clear mandate; loses effectiveness in ambiguous authority structures. Will escalate rather than absorb — prefers explicit resolution.

**Influence style:** Structured argument, clear asks, follows up in writing. Builds influence through reliability — people trust them because they do what they said. Less comfortable with impression management.

**Failure mode:** Optimises the machine for its own sake. Becomes change-resistant as operational complexity grows. Can suppress innovation by applying execution standards to exploratory work.

**SCARF profile:** Fairness: very high · Certainty: very high · Autonomy: moderate · Status: moderate · Relatedness: moderate

---

### CHRO / CPO
**Optimises for:** Organisational health and capability. Structurally positioned as the conscience of the system.

**Primary driver:** Organisational health and capability. Success feels like a place people want to work in, where talent sticks and the culture delivers the strategy.

**Primary threat:** Being the last person brought into decisions with people consequences. Carrying problems other leaders created. Having soft influence but hard accountability.

**Boundary behaviour:** Holds firm on ethics, legal exposure, and psychological safety. May give ground on style and process. Often has to hold unpopular lines without formal authority — this is the defining challenge of the role.

**Influence style:** Works through trust and private conversation more than formal authority. Uses data (engagement scores, attrition patterns) to make the intangible tangible. Most effective when paired with a CEO who publicly backs them.

**Failure mode:** Becomes HR-as-compliance rather than HR-as-strategy. Absorbs all the organisational pain without surfacing root causes. Starts speaking only to the CEO and loses the room.

**SCARF profile:** Relatedness: very high · Fairness: very high · Status: moderate (often underweighted by peers) · Certainty: moderate · Autonomy: moderate

## Part 4: The Decision Map — Who to Approach for What

Choosing the wrong person first is one of the most common mistakes — not because they'll say no, but because approaching them out of sequence can trigger status threats in the person you actually need.

> **Approaching in the wrong sequence is itself a status signal — it tells people where they rank in your mind.**

| Decision type | Who decides | Who must input | Who must be told | Who to brief first |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic direction | CEO + board | All C-suite | Whole org | CFO, then CTO |
| Budget allocation | CFO + CEO | COO, dept heads | All affected | CFO first, always |
| Product / technology | CTO + CPO | CFO, CMO | COO, CEO | CTO early |
| People / org change | CEO + CHRO | Affected leads | Whole org | CHRO, then CEO |
| External comms / brand | CMO + CEO | Legal, CTO | All C-suite | CMO first |

## Part 5: The Interaction Playbook

### CEO

**Opening move:** Lead with the strategic stakes, not the operational detail. They're running many threads — tell them which thread this belongs to before anything else.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"This affects our trajectory on X"*
- *"I wanted you to hear this before it becomes a board question"*
- *"The decision that needs to sit with you is…"*

**Do:** Connect your ask to the narrative they're managing. Show you've considered the board view. Be concise — they're time-constrained and will fill gaps themselves.

**Don't:** Bring problems without a proposed direction. Don't ambush them in a group setting with something they haven't heard. Don't compete with their framing — redirect from inside it.

**When they push back:** Don't counter immediately. Pause, then ask what outcome they're most trying to protect. Their pushback is almost always about a risk they haven't said out loud yet.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** If you've triggered threat mode — usually by implying loss of control or narrative — restore certainty first. "Here's what we can guarantee" before "here's what we're proposing."

---

### CFO

**Opening move:** Lead with numbers or risk framing. If you don't have the numbers yet, say so explicitly and give a date. Ambiguity without a resolution path is their biggest trigger.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"The model shows…"*
- *"The downside scenario is bounded by…"*
- *"I'd like your input before we commit anything externally"*

**Do:** Bring them in early — before decisions are made, not after. Show your working. Acknowledge uncertainty in your numbers before they find it themselves.

**Don't:** Frame things emotionally or with urgency pressure. Don't go around them to the CEO on financial matters — they will find out and it permanently damages trust. Don't dismiss their concerns as being overly cautious.

**When they push back:** CFO pushback is usually a request for more certainty, not an actual no. Ask: "What would you need to see to feel comfortable with this?" Then deliver exactly that.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** If you've lost their trust (missed a commitment, surprised them with a cost), the only restoration path is radical transparency going forward. One proactive disclosure is worth ten reactive ones.

---

### CTO

**Opening move:** Give context before the ask. They hate being handed a conclusion — they want to understand the problem first and reach the solution themselves if possible.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"I wanted to understand the technical constraints before we set any timelines"*
- *"What would you need to be confident about this?"*
- *"Help me understand what we'd be trading off"*

**Do:** Respect their need to think before responding — don't require an answer in the room. Send pre-reads. Show that you've thought about technical implications even if imperfectly.

**Don't:** Set timelines before scope is agreed. Don't make technology decisions in rooms they weren't in. Don't frame technical debt as "just a cleanup item" — to them it's structural integrity.

**When they push back:** CTO pushback is often a feasibility concern they haven't yet fully articulated. Ask them to walk you through the risk. They usually become more collaborative once they feel you genuinely understand the constraint.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** If you've bypassed them on a technical decision, the repair is to give them meaningful input on what comes next — not an apology, but a genuine ask for their expertise on the consequent problem.

---

### CMO

**Opening move:** Acknowledge the external environment they're navigating before your ask. They live in a world where perception is reality — show you understand that context.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"Given where the brand sits right now…"*
- *"I want to make sure you're credited for the impact here"*
- *"What's the narrative risk if we do X?"*

**Do:** Give them visibility and attribution. Include them in strategic conversations early — they're often brought in at the execution stage when they should have shaped the strategy. Build the alliance before you need it.

**Don't:** Cut their budget in a way that looks like a judgment on their performance without a private conversation first. Don't make public-facing decisions without looping them in. Don't ask them to justify brand investment in pure ROI terms without acknowledging the measurement limits.

**When they push back:** CMO pushback is often about brand coherence or attribution. Ask them to explain the positioning risk — they usually have a specific external signal in mind that you haven't seen.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** Public credit is the highest-value repair. Acknowledging their contribution in a room that matters to them resets the status account quickly.

---

### COO

**Opening move:** Be precise and structured. Lead with what you need, by when, and why it matters operationally. They have low tolerance for vague asks.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"The specific output I need is…"*
- *"The deadline is X because of Y"*
- *"Here's what I've already checked off before coming to you"*

**Do:** Honour process — follow the agreed channels. Confirm things in writing after conversations. When you ask for something, specify the format and timeline. Be reliable — they extend trust to people who do what they said.

**Don't:** Create process workarounds, even with good intent — it reads as disrespect for the system they maintain. Don't bring the same problem twice without showing what changed. Don't escalate over their head without telling them first.

**When they push back:** COO pushback is usually about resource, sequencing, or role clarity. Ask: "Is this a resourcing question or a prioritisation question?" Then resolve the specific constraint rather than trying to persuade.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** Reliability is the currency. After a breakdown, the fastest repair is demonstrating you've addressed the root cause — not apologising, but showing it won't happen again and why.

---

### CHRO / CPO

**Opening move:** Be honest about the people dimension of what you're doing before they have to extract it. They already know — they're testing whether you know.

**Phrases that work:**
- *"I want to think through the people implications before we finalise this"*
- *"What am I not seeing here from a culture standpoint?"*
- *"I'd rather you tell me the hard thing than find out later"*

**Do:** Treat them as a strategic partner, not a support function. Bring them in on decisions with human consequences before those consequences are locked in. Use them to pressure-test your assumptions about how people will respond.

**Don't:** Treat CHRO conversations as HR admin. Don't make org changes and ask them to "handle the comms" — they need to shape the decision, not just the announcement. Don't dismiss engagement data as "soft" in their presence.

**When they push back:** CHRO pushback is almost always a warning signal about cultural or legal risk you haven't fully seen. Take it seriously even when the framing feels intangible — they're usually translating something specific from the organisation into abstract language.

**Restoring a damaged interaction:** If you've excluded them from something with people consequences, the repair is a direct conversation acknowledging the gap and explicitly asking for their view now, even retroactively. Being brought in early is itself the signal.

## Part 6: The Meta-Rules

### Political capital is a finite resource

Every time you call in a favour, override someone, or win a contested decision, you spend some. Every time you give someone a win, protect them publicly, or make them look good to their peers, you earn some. Track it mentally — some players are almost never in deficit because they spend carefully.

### Sequence matters as much as content

The CFO hearing your proposal for the first time in a room with the CEO is a different interaction from the CFO having shaped the proposal privately beforehand. Same idea, completely different response. Pre-wiring is not manipulation — it's respect for people's need to process before performing.

### The real negotiation is almost never the visible one

When two executives are in conflict over a budget line, they're usually actually in conflict over whose domain has more strategic importance. When a CTO resists a deadline, they're usually protecting a certainty or autonomy need. Solving the surface problem without addressing the underlying need just moves the conflict somewhere else.

### Silence is data

When someone goes quiet — stops contributing in meetings, stops responding promptly, gives one-word answers — that's a threat response, not indifference. Ask: which of their SCARF dimensions was last activated, and by whom?

### You can only push back effectively from a place of prior trust

Challenge from someone you've built credit with reads as caring. The same challenge from someone you haven't reads as aggression. This is why relationships matter before you need them, not when you need them.

### The best pushback is curious, not adversarial

"Help me understand how this works if X happens" is psychologically safer for the person being challenged and more likely to produce genuine reconsideration than a direct objection. People defend positions they're told are wrong; they reconsider positions when invited to stress-test them.

## Part 7: Quick Reference — In-Session Diagnostic

| If you see this… | It probably means… | Try this… |
|---|---|---|
| Repeating the same point louder | Status or certainty threat | Acknowledge their core concern explicitly before responding |
| Asking for more data when data exists | Delay tactic / threat mode | Ask what specific uncertainty the data would resolve |
| Going quiet after a proposal | Processing, or discomfort unsaid | Follow up 1:1 before the next group session |
| Sudden escalation to the CEO | Role boundary felt violated | Address the boundary directly, privately |
| Enthusiastic agreement that doesn't convert to action | Relatedness-driven yes, no real buy-in | Go back and ask what they'd need to actually commit |
| Blame directed at another function | Fairness threat, likely historical | Don't adjudicate — ask what a good outcome looks like for them |

## Part 8: Predictable Tensions Between Roles

These conflicts aren't personal — they're structural. Understanding them prevents you from getting drawn into the wrong level of the problem.

**CFO vs CMO** is almost always a measurement language conflict. The CFO wants attribution; the CMO is working in systems where attribution is genuinely uncertain. Neither is wrong — the gap is epistemic.

**CTO vs COO** is a speed/quality trade-off that has no permanent resolution, only ongoing negotiation cadence.

**CEO vs everyone** at moments of strategic pivot: the CEO's certainty need (must project confidence) conflicts with everyone else's certainty need (must know what's actually decided).

**CHRO vs the room** is a structural tension — they hold accountability for culture but rarely hold the formal power to enforce it.

---

## The Central Principle

Fix the conditions, and the content almost takes care of itself.

Most decisions that feel like substance conflicts are actually condition conflicts — people who don't feel safe, credited, or certain enough to engage properly with the actual question. When you learn to read the conditions before reaching for the content, the simulation (and the organisation) becomes considerably more navigable.
